# Quality Rubric

10 dimensions for scoring articles. Onboard seeds the reference examples from exemplar articles in memories/raw/articles/ (pulled from the person's favorites and the best writing in their space).

Score each dimension 1–5. An article is publication-ready when all dimensions are ≥ 4.

---

## 1. Reframe Opening

**Does the first paragraph change how the reader thinks about something?**

The best articles don't start by describing what they'll discuss. They correct a misconception or reframe a familiar concept.

- 5: Reader's mental model shifts in the first paragraph
- 4: Opens with a clear, non-obvious claim
- 3: Opens with a reasonable but unsurprising thesis
- 2: Opens with a summary of what the article covers
- 1: Opens with throat-clearing ("In this article, we'll explore...")

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — 2–3 openings from exemplar articles that do this well]

## 2. Name the Unnamed

**Does the article give vocabulary to patterns people recognize but can't articulate?**

The most durable articles introduce terms or frameworks that practitioners adopt in conversation.

- 5: Introduces a framework or term that will enter the reader's vocabulary
- 4: Gives clear names to one or two previously fuzzy concepts
- 3: Organizes known concepts but doesn't introduce new language
- 2: Uses existing terminology without adding clarity
- 1: Vague throughout — no crisp definitions

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — named frameworks or terms from exemplar articles]

---

## 3. Early Concreteness

**Are there real products, numbers, or scenarios in the first 20 lines?**

Abstractions without anchors don't land. Ground claims in specifics early.

- 5: Multiple concrete examples (named products, specific numbers) within the first 20 lines
- 4: At least one concrete example or number in the opening section
- 3: Concrete examples appear but not until mid-article
- 2: Examples are generic or hypothetical
- 1: Pure abstraction — no real-world anchors

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — opening passages with strong concrete anchors]

---

## 4. Structured Compression

**Is there at least one structural element (table, list, blockquote) worth screenshotting?**

The best articles compress their argument into a referenceable artifact.

- 5: Contains a table, diagram, or structured list that a reader would screenshot and share
- 4: Has clear structural elements that aid scanning and reference
- 3: Uses formatting but it's decorative, not compressive
- 2: Wall of prose with minimal structure
- 1: No structural aids

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — screenshot-worthy tables, lists, or diagrams from exemplar articles]

---

## 5. Earned Product Mention

**Does the insight come before the pitch?**

Product references should feel like natural consequences of the argument, not the reason for the article.

- 5: Article would be worth reading with all product mentions removed; products appear as natural examples after the insight
- 4: Product mentions are relevant and positioned after value delivery
- 3: Product is mentioned early but doesn't dominate
- 2: Article reads like a product announcement dressed as thought leadership
- 1: The entire article is a product pitch

**Test:** Remove every product/company mention. Is the article still worth reading? If yes → ≥ 4.

---

## 6. Layered Accessibility

**Do multiple audience roles (e.g., PM, engineer, exec) each get value from the same article?**

- 5: Three distinct audience layers each get actionable value
- 4: Two audience layers clearly served
- 3: Written for one audience, others can follow but don't get specific value
- 2: Requires significant domain expertise to follow
- 1: Inaccessible to anyone outside one narrow role

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — articles that work for multiple audiences with notes on which parts land for whom]

---

## 7. Assertive Brevity

**Are the core claims short, declarative, and tweetable?**

When making the central point, use short sentences with no hedging.

- 5: 2-3 sentences could be pulled out and work as standalone tweets
- 4: Core claims are clear and direct
- 3: Points are made but buried in qualifying language
- 2: Important claims hedged or softened unnecessarily
- 1: No single sentence captures the article's point

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — short declarative sentences pulled from exemplar articles that carry the whole argument]

---

## 8. External References

**Does the article cite ≥ 3 sources outside its own ecosystem?**

Referencing external work positions the author as part of a conversation, not a marketing voice.

- 5: References ≥ 5 external sources (competitors, papers, other companies' products)
- 4: References 3-4 external sources
- 3: References 1-2 external sources
- 2: Only references own products/company
- 1: No external references

**Reference examples:** [seeded by onboard — articles that cite across the field rather than just their own ecosystem]

---

## 9. One Memorable Thesis

**Can the reader retell the argument in one sentence a week later?**

- 5: Thesis is so clear and sharp it becomes a reference point in the reader's thinking
- 4: Thesis is clear and memorable after one read
- 3: Thesis exists but competes with secondary points
- 2: Multiple points, no clear winner
- 1: Can't identify what the article is arguing

## 10. Right-Sized Length

**Is every paragraph earning its place? Could you cut 20% without losing anything?**

- 5: Impossibly tight — every paragraph advances the argument, nothing is filler
- 4: One or two paragraphs could be tighter but nothing is wasted
- 3: Some sections feel padded or repetitive
- 2: Significant filler — article could be half the length
- 1: Mostly padding around a thin idea

**Length calibration (typical ranges):**
- Strategic argument / sharp take: ~800 words
- Definitional / explainer post: ~1200 words
- Framework post: ~1800 words
- Recipe / process post: ~2000 words
- Opinion / extended argument post: ~2500 words

Match length to argument complexity. If a 500-word insight is stretched to 2000 words, this dimension fails.
