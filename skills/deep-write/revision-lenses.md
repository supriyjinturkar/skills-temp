# Revision Lenses

Each pass through the loop uses one lens. Read the draft through that lens ONLY — don't get distracted fixing things that belong to a different lens. Focused passes produce better results than unfocused ones.

## Lens 1: Structure & Argument

**Question: Is the logic tight?**

Read for the skeleton of the argument, ignoring prose quality.

Check:
- Does the opening establish the reframe? (Rubric #1)
- Does each section advance the thesis, or is it a tangent?
- Is there a clear thesis → evidence → implication arc? (Rubric #9)
- Are sections in the right order? Would reordering strengthen the flow?
- Does the conclusion land somewhere new, or just restate the opening?

Common fixes:
- Cut sections that don't serve the thesis
- Reorder to put the strongest evidence earlier
- Merge sections that make the same point from different angles
- Sharpen the opening if it buries the reframe
- Add an implication or "what this means" section if the article just stops

## Lens 2: Evidence & Concreteness

**Question: Are claims grounded?**

Read for every claim, assertion, or abstraction. Each one needs an anchor.

Check:
- Is there a concrete example (named product, specific number, real scenario) within the first 20 lines? (Rubric #3)
- Does every abstract claim have supporting evidence within 2 paragraphs?
- Are there ≥ 3 external references? (Rubric #8)
- Is there a structural compression artifact — a table, numbered list, or blockquote worth screenshotting? (Rubric #4)
- Are product mentions earned — do they come after the insight, not before? (Rubric #5)

Common fixes:
- Replace vague magnitudes ("significant improvement") with specific numbers
- Add named products, papers, or companies as evidence
- Build a comparison table to compress a multi-part argument
- Move product mentions downstream — teach first, then reference
- Add a web search to fill evidence gaps (save sources to memories/raw/articles/)
- Verify factual claims against source material in memories/raw/ and memories/wiki/

## Lens 3: Voice & Readability

**Question: Does it sound right?**

Read aloud (mentally). Check for flow, tone, and the anti-patterns in writing-voice.

Check:
- Does it sound like something this person would publish? Load writing-voice for reference.
- Are core claims short and declarative? (Rubric #7)
- Are paragraphs short (1-4 sentences)?
- Is there any filler from the anti-filler checklist? (writing-voice)
- Does it work for multiple audience layers? (Rubric #6)
- Is technical jargon explained when writing for broader audiences, or left clean when writing for builders?

Common fixes:
- Delete throat-clearing ("In this article we'll explore...")
- Shorten sentences that carry the core argument
- Break long paragraphs
- Remove hedging language from central claims
- Add a brief parenthetical or link when using terms that might exclude non-technical readers
- Cut whatever writing-voice flags (emojis, hashtags, exclamation points, etc.)

## Lens 4: Compression

**Question: Can anything be cut?**

This is the most aggressive lens. Read with a knife.

Check:
- Could you cut 20% without losing any argument? (Rubric #10)
- Are there sentences that say what the next sentence will say?
- Are there sections that make the same point as another section?
- Is the length matched to the argument complexity? (see rubric #10 calibration)
- Are there qualifying phrases that don't add precision? ("It's worth noting that...", "Interestingly...")

Common fixes:
- Delete every sentence that announces what the next sentence will say
- Merge sections with overlapping points
- Replace 3-sentence explanations with 1 crisp sentence
- Cut the conclusion if it just restates the opening
- Remove "to be sure" / "of course" / "naturally" qualifiers unless they genuinely add nuance

## Lens Cycling

After completing all 4 lenses in order, check: did the last pass through any lens result in changes? If yes, cycle again. If no lens produced changes, the article has converged.

Typical convergence: 1-2 full cycles (4-8 total passes). If you're past 3 cycles without converging, the problem is likely the frame — step back and re-evaluate the thesis.
