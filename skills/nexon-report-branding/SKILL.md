# Nexon Report Branding

This skill defines the recurring visual language seen across the deidentified Nexon service-report decks and should be used for weekly, monthly, quarterly, and other period-based reporting.

## Use this skill for

- designing a new periodic report deck
- re-skinning a draft so it matches Nexon reporting expectations
- checking whether a PPT feels visually consistent with the existing Nexon deck family
- keeping charts, tables, commentary blocks, and divider slides coherent across authors

## What to read

- For the observed deck evidence and exact palette/theme notes:
  - `references/observed-brand-patterns.md`

## Core brand rules

- Use a 16:9 widescreen slide layout.
- Default to `Arial` across titles, body text, tables, and chart labels unless an approved Nexon master explicitly overrides it.
- Keep the deck bright and presentation-led:
  - white or very light backgrounds
  - dark neutral body text
  - accent color used to guide attention rather than to flood the slide
- Treat the observed theme accents as the working palette:
  - primary blue `#2E8AF4`
  - alert red `#E34749`
  - caution amber `#FFBF00`
  - healthy green `#00C982`
  - supporting light blue `#87C9F7`
  - reserved soft pink `#FA9EAB`
- Use color semantically:
  - blue for primary emphasis or headline visuals
  - red for breaches, risk, or negative exceptions
  - amber for caution or watch items
  - green for healthy or on-target results
- Keep the confidentiality footer visible on recurring body slides when the deck format allows it.
- Keep slide numbers visible and unobtrusive.

## Slide construction rules

- One slide should carry one main message.
- Prefer one visual, one table, or one concise list per slide.
- Use headlines that state the subject directly, not clever labels.
- When interpretation is needed, pair the evidence with a short commentary block instead of adding dense prose around the visual.
- If detail overflows, continue it on the next slide or move it to an appendix slide. Do not shrink text to force too much onto one page.
- Use section-divider slides to reset the audience between major themes.
- Keep whitespace intentional. These decks read better when the eye can locate the title, evidence, and commentary quickly.

## Chart and table rules

- Favor clean KPI cards, simple trend views, RAG/status tables, and operational detail tables over decorative graphics.
- Label tables and charts so a service manager can understand them without narration.
- Use continuation slides for long lists such as breach details, backlog detail, or incident tables.
- Keep legends, labels, and headers readable at presentation scale.
- Do not use color-only encoding when a label, icon, or heading can make the meaning clearer.

## Tone rules

- The deck should feel operational, composed, and evidence-led.
- Commentary should sound factual and calm, even when surfacing risks.
- Avoid marketing language, novelty layouts, and heavy visual ornament.
- Aim for consistency across customers so the service narrative changes by account, not by slide style.
